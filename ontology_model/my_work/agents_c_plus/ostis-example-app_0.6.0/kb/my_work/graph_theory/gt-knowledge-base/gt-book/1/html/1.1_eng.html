Definitions and examples

The graph G = (V, E) consists of two sets: a finite set of elements called vertices and a finite set of elements called ribs. Each edge is defined by a pair of vertices. If the edges of the graph are defined by ordered pairs of vertices, then G is called a directional or directed graph. Otherwise G is undirected or undirected graph. To indicate the vertices of the graph will use the symbols v 1, v 2, v 3, ..., and to refer to the ribs - E 1, E 2, E 3. . . . The vertices v i and v j, determining an edge e i, called the end vertices of e i. In this case, the edge e is denoted as i e i = (v i, v j). Note that the set E may be more than one edge with the same end vertices. All edges have the same end vertices are called parallel. Furthermore, the end vertices of the edge is not necessarily different. If e i = (v i, v i), the edge e i is called a loop. A graph is simple if it does not contain loops and parallel edges. A graph G is a graph of order n, if the set of vertices consists of n elements.

Graf, has no edges, called empty. A graph that has no vertices (and hence, ribs), called null-graph .

Graphically, the graph can be represented by a diagram which shows the tip of a point or a circle, and the edge - a line segment connecting the dots or circles, corresponding to the end vertices of the edge.
